Objection-Handling Quick Reference

Five common objections and how to respond — Free Leasing Training template

1. "It's more than I wanted to spend."

What it usually means
They're not sure the price matches the value — not necessarily that the number itself is too high.
Try this
Acknowledge first: "That's fair, let's talk about it." Then reconnect the price to what they said mattered to them on the tour — commute, space, amenities — instead of just defending the number.

2. "I want to see other places first."

What it usually means
They liked what they saw, but don't feel urgency to decide yet.
Try this
Don't push back. Mention any real, factual urgency that exists (limited availability, a move-in special ending) — never manufactured pressure. Offer to follow up at a specific time.

3. "I need to talk to my partner / roommate."

What it usually means
A legitimate part of their decision process — not a brush-off.
Try this
Make it easy: offer to answer the other person's questions directly, or invite them to see the unit too. Don't treat this as a dead end.

4. "I'm just looking."

What it usually means
Often true early in a search — but after a tour that seemed to go well, it can be a polite way to end a conversation that didn't fully land.
Try this
Gently ask what would need to be true for the place to feel right for them. The answer often reveals an objection you can actually address.

5. "I'm not sure this is the right fit."

What it usually means
Something specific is bothering them that they haven't named yet.
Try this
Ask directly: "What's giving you pause?" Don't fill the silence with reassurance before you know what you're actually addressing.
An objection is not a debate to win. If you find yourself arguing a prospect out of their concern rather than answering it, you've shifted from selling to convincing — and convincing rarely produces a resident who's happy they signed.
