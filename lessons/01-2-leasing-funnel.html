The leasing funnel — how prospects become residents

10 min

In the last lesson, we said leasing is sales, not customer service — and that means managing a funnel, not just answering questions. Let's break that funnel down stage by stage, so you always know exactly what you're trying to accomplish, and exactly what "winning" looks like at each point.

Stage 1: Inquiry

A prospect calls, emails, texts, or fills out a form on a listing site. At this stage, your only goal is to get them excited enough to book a tour. You are not trying to close them over the phone — you're trying to earn 30 minutes of their time in person (or on a video call).

The biggest mistake at this stage is over-explaining. A prospect who emails asking about pricing doesn't need a paragraph about every floor plan you offer — they need a fast, friendly response that answers their question and immediately offers a tour time. Speed matters more than thoroughness here: prospects are usually contacting several properties at once, and the first agent to respond often has a real advantage before anyone even shows up in person.

Success at this stage looks like: a booked tour, with a specific date and time confirmed — not just "let me know if you want to come by."

Stage 2: Tour

This is where most leases are actually won or lost. The tour isn't a walkthrough of square footage — it's where the prospect decides whether they can picture themselves living there. Your job is to listen for what they actually care about (commute, space, pets, price) and tailor what you show and say around that.

Two prospects touring the exact same unit should get two different tours. Someone who mentioned working from home should hear about the natural light in the second bedroom and the building's internet speed. Someone who mentioned a dog should hear about the nearest park and the pet policy before they even ask. Generic tours get generic results; tailored tours convert.

Success at this stage looks like: the prospect leaves with a clear next step — either they've expressed interest in applying, or you've agreed on exactly what needs to happen next (e.g., "I'll see the second unit you mentioned and follow up tomorrow").

Stage 3: Application

A prospect who tours and likes the unit doesn't always apply on the spot. This stage is about removing friction — making sure they understand the process, the cost, the timeline, and that there's nothing confusing standing between "I like this place" and "I've submitted my application."

This is often where good prospects quietly disappear — not because they changed their mind, but because the application felt unclear or intimidating (unexpected fees, unclear documentation requirements, no sense of how long approval takes). Walk every prospect through what to expect before they start the application, not after they hit a confusing step and abandon it.

Success at this stage looks like: a submitted, complete application — with the prospect already knowing what to expect next and roughly when they'll hear back.

Stage 4: Signed lease

The application gets approved and the lease gets signed. Your job here is mostly logistics — but it's also your last chance to set expectations clearly so the move-in goes smoothly and the resident starts the relationship on a good note.

Agents sometimes treat this stage as "done" the moment the lease is signed, but a resident's experience between signing and move-in shapes how they feel about the whole property. Confirm move-in dates, what they need to bring or set up beforehand, and who to contact with questions — proactively, not just when asked.

Success at this stage looks like: a signed lease and a resident who knows exactly what happens between now and move-in day.

Why this matters

Every stage has a different goal. Treating a phone inquiry like a closing conversation — or treating a tour like a Q&A session instead of an experience — is why prospects drop out of the funnel. Know which stage you're in, and know what "success" looks like at that specific stage.

This also gives you a way to diagnose your own performance. If you're getting plenty of inquiries but few tours, the problem is in stage 1. If tours are happening but applications aren't, the problem is in stage 2 (or in how you're guiding stage 3). Knowing the stages lets you find the leak instead of just feeling like "leasing is slow right now."
