Handling inquiries — phone, email, and chat

9 min

Every lease starts with an inquiry — a call, an email, a text, or a chat widget message. How you handle those first few minutes (or sentences) determines whether a prospect books a tour or quietly moves on to the next listing on their screen. This lesson covers how to handle each channel well.

Speed is the single biggest lever

Prospects rarely inquire about just one property. By the time you respond, they may have already heard back from two competitors. Studies on lead response consistently show conversion drops sharply after the first few minutes of delay. If you can't respond immediately, even a quick "got your message, calling you in 10 minutes" holds the lead far better than silence.

Practically: check inboxes and missed calls between tours, not just at the start and end of your shift. A same-day response is not fast enough if a competitor responded in five minutes.

Phone inquiries

Phone is the highest-value channel because it's a real conversation — use it. Don't just answer the question that was asked; use it to learn what the prospect actually needs.

  • Answer warmly and get their name early — it makes the rest of the call personal instead of transactional.
  • Ask one or two open questions before pitching anything: "What's bringing you to the area?" or "What's most important to you in your next place?"
  • Always end with a specific ask: a booked tour time, not "feel free to stop by."

Email inquiries

Email feels less urgent than a phone call, which is exactly why agents under-invest in it. Treat every email inquiry like it's your only shot at this prospect.

  • Answer the actual question first — don't bury pricing or availability under a generic template.
  • Keep it short. A wall of text about every amenity gets skimmed, not read.
  • End with a clear next step and make booking easy: offer two or three specific tour times rather than asking "when works for you?" (which adds a step they may not bother with).

Chat and text inquiries

These channels move fast and informally — match that tone, but don't let it become careless. Short, quick replies are appropriate, but every reply should still move the conversation toward a tour.

  • Respond like a real person, not a script — chat feels robotic fast if every answer sounds copy-pasted.
  • Don't let the conversation trail off. If a prospect goes quiet mid-conversation, a same-day follow-up text ("still happy to grab you a tour time whenever works!") keeps the door open.

What every channel has in common

Regardless of the channel, every inquiry response should do three things: answer what was actually asked, ask one question that helps you understand the prospect's needs, and end with a specific next step. Skipping any one of these is usually why an inquiry goes cold.
