Fair housing essentials — what every agent must know

12 min

Of everything in this curriculum, this is the lesson with zero room for "it depends." Fair housing law isn't a best practice — it's a legal requirement, and violations carry real consequences for you, your team, and the property. This lesson covers the essentials every agent needs internalized before taking a single call.

A note before we start: this lesson covers general fair housing principles under U.S. federal law (the Fair Housing Act) for training purposes. It is not legal advice. Many states and cities add their own protected classes on top of the federal list — always confirm the specific requirements for your market with your employer or legal counsel.

The federally protected classes

Under the Fair Housing Act, it's illegal to discriminate in housing based on:

  • Race
  • Color
  • National origin
  • Religion
  • Sex (including sexual orientation and gender identity)
  • Familial status (having children, or being pregnant)
  • Disability

Many states and cities add additional protected classes — common examples include age, marital status, source of income (e.g. housing vouchers), and military status. Know what applies in your specific market; "I didn't know that was protected here" is not a defense.

What discrimination actually looks like in practice

Most fair housing violations aren't dramatic — they're small, often well-intentioned comments that steer prospects toward or away from a property based on a protected class. A few real patterns to watch for in yourself:

  • Steering: suggesting a prospect look at a "quieter" building or a different neighborhood based on assumptions about who they are, rather than what they actually asked for.
  • Different treatment: requiring extra documentation, a larger deposit, or a co-signer from some applicants but not others in similar financial situations.
  • Discouraging family households: implying a unit "isn't really set up for kids," or asking how many children someone has when screening, instead of just stating the lawful occupancy limit for the unit.
  • Failure to accommodate: denying a reasonable accommodation request (like a request for an assistance animal despite a no-pets policy, or a request for an accessible parking spot) without a legitimate basis.

Notice that none of these require malicious intent. Fair housing complaints often come from agents who believed they were being helpful — "I just thought they'd be happier somewhere else." Intent doesn't matter; impact does.

Language that keeps you compliant

The safest approach is simple: describe the property and the policy, not the people. A few practical swaps:

  • Instead of "this building is great for young professionals" → "this building has a coworking lounge and is close to downtown."
  • Instead of asking "are these kids in school yet?" → simply state the lawful occupancy limit for the unit.
  • Instead of "I don't think this would be a good fit for you" → present the same options and information to every prospect, and let them decide fit for themselves.

If you wouldn't say it to every single prospect regardless of how they look, what they sound like, or what they mention about their life — don't say it at all.

Advertising matters too

Fair housing rules don't just apply to conversations — they apply to listings, photos, and ads. Avoid language that signals a preference for (or against) any protected class, even subtly — phrases like "perfect for a single person" or "no kids" are red flags, even when they seem like throwaway marketing copy.

When in doubt

If a prospect asks something that puts you in a gray area — a question about the neighborhood's demographics, a request you're not sure is a "reasonable accommodation," anything that feels off — it's always appropriate to say "let me check on that and follow up," rather than improvising an answer. Loop in your property manager or compliance contact. Getting it right matters more than answering instantly.
