Unit 4 · After the lease

Move-in experience and first impressions

9 min

The sale doesn't end when the lease is signed. Move-in day is the resident's first real experience of actually living there — and it shapes how they feel about the property for the entire length of their lease, including whether they renew. This lesson covers how to make that first impression count.

The gap between signing and move-in matters

There's often a stretch of days or weeks between signing a lease and the actual move-in date. Residents can get anxious in that gap if they don't hear from anyone — wondering if everything's actually set, what they need to bring, or who to contact if something comes up. A short check-in message partway through that gap (confirming the date, sharing move-in instructions) keeps that anxiety from building.

Make sure the unit is actually ready

This sounds obvious, but it's the single most common way first impressions go wrong. Walk the unit yourself before the resident arrives — confirm cleaning is done, repairs from the previous resident's move-out are finished, and anything promised during the leasing process (a specific appliance, a fresh coat of paint) is actually in place. A resident who walks into an unfinished unit on day one starts the relationship feeling misled, even if it gets fixed quickly afterward.

Be present, or make sure someone is

Whether it's you or someone else on the team, having a person available on move-in day — to hand over keys, answer last-minute questions, and point out where things are (mailbox, trash, parking, maintenance request process) — makes the day feel supported instead of transactional. A resident fumbling to figure out basic logistics alone on day one remembers that.

Set expectations for what comes next

Tell new residents exactly how to submit a maintenance request, who to contact for questions, and what to expect in their first few weeks (e.g. a welcome call, a follow-up survey). Residents who know how things work are far less likely to feel frustrated when something inevitably needs fixing.

The small gestures that aren't actually small

A welcome note, a small gift, or simply a personal "welcome home" message on move-in day costs very little but has an outsized effect on how a resident remembers their first day. People remember being made to feel welcome — not because it's required, but because it's rare.

Why this connects back to leasing

A resident who feels good about move-in is a resident who's more likely to renew, more likely to leave a good review, and more likely to refer a friend — all of which make your job easier down the line. The leasing relationship doesn't end at signing; it just changes phase.
