Unit 2 · The prospect journey

The tour experience — showing units that convert

11 min

The tour is the moment of truth. A prospect who books a tour is genuinely interested — what happens in those 20-30 minutes determines whether that interest turns into an application. This lesson covers how to run a tour that actually converts.

Start before they arrive

Walk the unit beforehand. Lights on, blinds open, no maintenance tape or leftover supplies sitting around. A unit that looks cared-for signals that the whole property is well-managed — and a unit that looks rushed undercuts everything you say during the tour.

Lead with questions, not a script

Don't open with a memorized walkthrough. Ask what's bringing them to the search, what they didn't like about their current place, or what matters most in the new one. Their answers tell you exactly what to emphasize — and skipping this step means you're guessing instead of knowing.

If someone mentions they work from home, walk them past the spot that gets the best natural light before you mention the gym. If someone mentions a dog, bring up the nearest park unprompted. Tailoring the tour to what they actually told you is what separates a memorable tour from a generic one.

Sell the lifestyle, not just the square footage

Prospects aren't just renting a floor plan — they're picturing a life. Instead of "this is the kitchen," try "this is where you'll probably end up making coffee every morning." Small framing shifts like this help people visualize themselves living there, which is what actually drives a decision.

Address concerns directly, don't dodge them

If a prospect points out something less than perfect — older appliances, a smaller closet — don't ignore it or talk over it. Acknowledge it honestly, and pivot to what offsets it. Prospects trust agents who are straightforward far more than ones who pretend everything is flawless.

End with a clear next step, every time

Never end a tour with "let me know if you're interested." Ask directly: "What did you think?" Listen to the answer, then guide them toward the next concrete step — starting an application, seeing a second unit, or getting a question answered that's holding them back. A tour without a clear next step is a tour that's likely to go cold.
