Reading buying signals

8 min

Prospects rarely announce "I've decided to apply." Instead, they give off smaller signals — in what they say, what they ask, and how they behave — that show where they actually stand. Learning to read these signals lets you respond to where the prospect really is, instead of guessing.

Verbal signals

Listen for language that shifts from hypothetical to possessive. "Where would the couch go?" or "How far is the dog park from here?" is a very different signal than "What's the square footage?" — the first is someone picturing themselves living there, the second is someone still gathering facts.

Questions about logistics — move-in timing, lease length, what's included in rent — are also strong signals. People don't usually ask about move-in dates unless they're seriously considering one.

Behavioral signals

Pay attention to how someone moves through the space, not just what they say. Lingering in a room, opening closets and cabinets unprompted, or taking photos are all signs of real interest — window-shoppers tend to move quickly and ask fewer questions.

Bringing someone else along — a partner, roommate, or parent — to a second tour is one of the strongest signals there is. People don't usually involve other decision-makers unless they're seriously close to committing.

Signals that someone is stalling, not deciding

Not all hesitation is the same. "I need to think about it" paired with specific follow-up questions usually means real consideration. "I need to think about it" with no questions and a vague, fast exit often means something is holding them back that they haven't said out loud — which is usually an unaddressed objection, covered in the next lesson.

What to do once you spot a signal

Don't just notice the signal — act on it. If someone asks about move-in timing, that's your moment to ask directly: "Are you thinking you'd want to move in around then?" Buying signals are an invitation to move the conversation forward, not something to passively observe.

What's next

Even an interested prospect often has something holding them back from committing. The next lesson covers how to recognize and respond to common objections.
