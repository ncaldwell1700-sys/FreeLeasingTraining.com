Overcoming objections

10 min

An objection isn't a "no" — it's usually a question in disguise. A prospect who says "it's a bit more than I wanted to spend" is often really asking "is this worth the extra cost?" Treating objections as something to overcome through pressure misses the point; treating them as something to understand and answer is what actually moves a prospect forward.

Step one: acknowledge before you respond

The instinct to immediately argue against an objection ("well actually, it's a great deal because...") makes prospects feel unheard and defensive. Acknowledge first — "that's fair, let's talk about it" — before addressing the substance. This small pause makes everything you say afterward land better.

The price objection

"It's more than I wanted to spend" is rarely about the number in isolation — it's about whether the prospect believes the price matches the value. Instead of just defending the price, reconnect it to what they said mattered to them on the tour: the commute, the space, the amenities they responded to. Value context changes how a price feels.

The "I want to see other places first" objection

This usually means the prospect liked what they saw but doesn't feel urgency yet. Rather than pushing back, give them a reason to decide sooner without sounding pushy — mention real, factual urgency if it exists (limited availability, a move-in special ending) rather than manufacturing pressure that doesn't exist.

The "I need to talk to my partner/roommate" objection

This is a legitimate part of someone's decision process, not a brush-off — don't treat it like one. Make it easy: offer to answer any questions the other decision-maker might have, or invite them to see the unit too. Removing friction from involving a second person often speeds up the decision rather than slowing it down.

The "I'm just looking" objection

Often this is true early in someone's search, but it can also be a polite way to end a conversation that didn't fully land. If you hear this after a tour that seemed to go well, it's worth gently asking what would need to be true for the place to feel right — the answer often reveals an unspoken objection you can actually address.

What objections are not

An objection is not a debate to win. If you find yourself arguing a prospect out of their concern rather than addressing it, you've shifted from selling to convincing — and convincing rarely produces a resident who's happy they signed. The goal is an honest answer, not a won argument.

What's next

Once an objection is resolved and a prospect is ready to move forward, the next step is the application itself — and making sure that process doesn't introduce new objections of its own.
