Unit 4 · After the lease

Renewals — keeping residents you already have

10 min

It's almost always cheaper and easier to keep a resident than to find a new one — between marketing spend, vacancy loss, and turnover costs, a renewal is one of the most valuable outcomes in leasing. Yet renewals often get far less attention than new leads. This lesson covers how to actually earn them.

Renewals are won long before the renewal conversation

By the time a lease is up for renewal, the decision has often already been shaped by everything that happened in between — how maintenance requests were handled, whether communication felt responsive, whether the resident felt valued or just billed every month. A renewal conversation can't undo a year of feeling ignored.

Start the conversation early

Reach out well before the lease end date — not just when the lease is about to expire. This gives the resident time to think it over without scrambling, and gives you time to address any concerns before they default to "I'll just look elsewhere."

Ask before you assume

Instead of opening with a renewal offer, ask how their experience has been. This surfaces problems you can actually fix (a maintenance issue, a noise complaint, a billing question) before the resident has mentally checked out. A resident who feels heard is far more likely to stay, even if rent is increasing.

Frame the increase, don't just announce it

If rent is increasing at renewal, don't just send a number. Briefly contextualize it — market conditions, any updates or improvements made to the property — so it doesn't feel arbitrary. Residents are usually more accepting of an increase they understand than a smaller one that feels unexplained.

Make staying easier than leaving

Moving is expensive, stressful, and time-consuming — that's already a point in your favor. Reinforce it: streamline the renewal paperwork, offer a quick turnaround, and remove any friction from saying "yes." A resident who's on the fence often stays simply because renewing was the path of least resistance.

Don't ignore residents who don't renew

When someone does decide to leave, ask why — directly and without being defensive. Exit feedback is some of the most honest information you'll get about what's actually working and not working at the property, and it can directly improve your renewal rate with future residents.
