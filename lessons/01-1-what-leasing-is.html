What leasing actually is (and isn't)

8 min

Most agents get handed a desk, a phone, and a property tour script — and that's the entire onboarding. Nobody actually explains what the job is. So let's start there.

Leasing is sales, not customer service

A lot of agents treat leasing like answering questions: someone calls, you tell them the price and square footage, and you wait to see if they apply. That's customer service, and it converts poorly.

Leasing is sales. Your job is to move a person from "curious" to "signed" — and that means guiding the conversation, not just responding to it. Every inquiry is an opportunity to ask questions, understand what the prospect actually needs, and position your property as the answer.

Here's the difference in practice. A customer-service mindset waits for the prospect to ask "does it have in-unit laundry?" and answers "yes." A sales mindset asks back: "Is laundry a must-have for you, or just nice to have?" — because the answer changes what you say next, and shows the prospect you're actually listening instead of reading off a spec sheet.

This doesn't mean being pushy. The best leasing agents don't sound like salespeople — they sound like a knowledgeable friend who happens to know everything about the property and the neighborhood. But underneath that friendliness is a deliberate goal: move this specific conversation one step closer to a signed lease.

You're managing a funnel, not a unit

Think of every prospect as moving through stages: inquiry → tour → application → signed lease. Your job changes at each stage. On a call, you're building enough interest to get them to tour. On a tour, you're building enough confidence to get them to apply. Confusing these stages — for example, trying to "close" someone on the first phone call — is one of the most common reasons agents underperform.

Here's a concrete way to think about it: at the inquiry stage, "winning" doesn't mean getting an application — it means getting a booked tour. If you try to skip ahead and sell the whole apartment over the phone, you'll either overwhelm the prospect with information they can't act on yet, or you'll burn through your best material before they've even seen the place. Save the close for the stage where closing actually happens.

We'll cover each of these stages — inquiry, tour, application, and what happens after signing — in detail in the lessons that follow this unit.

What leasing isn't

  • It isn't reciting amenities and waiting for a reaction.
  • It isn't being "nice" and hoping that's enough to convert.
  • It isn't gatekeeping information until someone applies.

That last one deserves a second look, because it's a trap a lot of agents fall into without realizing it. Some agents withhold pricing, availability, or details about the application process, thinking it protects their leverage — "if I tell them everything now, why would they need to talk to me again?" In practice, this just frustrates prospects and pushes them toward whichever competing property was more straightforward. Transparency builds trust, and trust is what actually gets leases signed.

The best agents are proactive, ask good questions, and treat every prospect like they're one good conversation away from becoming a resident — because often, they are.

A simple mental model

Before every interaction — a call, an email, a tour — ask yourself one question: what does this specific prospect need from me right now in order to take the next step? Sometimes that's information. Sometimes it's reassurance. Sometimes it's just a fast response so they don't move on to the next property on their list. Identifying that one thing, every time, is most of what separates strong agents from average ones.
