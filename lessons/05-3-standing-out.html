How to stand out as a leasing professional

10 min

Everything in this curriculum — handling inquiries well, running tours that convert, following up without being pushy, tracking your numbers — will already put you ahead of most agents, because most agents never learned any of it formally. This final lesson is about the layer on top of that: what actually makes an agent memorable, not just competent.

Competence gets you to "fine." Something else gets you to "great."

Most agents in most markets are reasonably competent — they answer questions, run tours, process applications. Competence is table stakes, not a differentiator. What separates the agents people remember and request by name is something less procedural: how they make people feel during a process that's often stressful.

Become a local expert, not just a property expert

Prospects aren't just choosing a unit — they're choosing a neighborhood, a commute, a lifestyle. Agents who can speak confidently about the area — the best coffee shop nearby, which bus actually gets downtown fastest, when the farmers market runs — give prospects something no listing photo can: a sense that someone who actually knows the area is helping them, not just reciting square footage.

Remember details, and use them

If a prospect mentioned their dog's name, their new job, or a moving timeline, bring it up again later. "How's the new job going?" in a follow-up message takes five seconds to write and signals that you were actually listening, not running through a script. This is one of the cheapest, most underused ways to stand out.

Be the agent who follows through

Say you'll follow up by Thursday — follow up by Thursday. Say you'll check on something — actually check, and report back even if the answer is "no update yet." A huge amount of distrust in the leasing process comes from agents who say things and don't follow through. Simply doing what you said you'd do, consistently, is rarer than it should be — and prospects notice.

Ask for feedback, and actually use it

Periodically ask residents and even prospects who didn't sign what worked and what didn't about their experience with you. Most agents never ask, which means they never improve past whatever habits they started with. A little structured feedback compounds over time into real skill.

Think beyond your current role

Agents who treat leasing as a stepping stone — toward property management, training, or running their own team — tend to take the fundamentals more seriously, because they're building a track record, not just getting through a shift. Whatever you want next, the way to get there starts with being excellent at what's in front of you right now.

You've completed the curriculum

That's the full arc — from understanding what leasing actually is, through every stage of the funnel, to what happens after the lease, to how you grow from here. Come back to any lesson whenever a real situation calls for it; that's what this is here for.
